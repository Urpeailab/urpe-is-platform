2.1 The Core Problem: Small Business Logistics Deficiencies and Supply Chain Fragility

The United States economy structurally depends on the operational capacity of its small business sector. According to the U.S. Small Business Administration's 2023 Small Business Profile, small businesses with fewer than 500 employees represent 99.9% of all U.S. firms and employ 45.9% of the private sector workforce (SBA Office of Advocacy, "2023 Small Business Profile," March 2023, https://advocacy.sba.gov/2023/03/07/2023-small-business-profile/).

These businesses form the connective tissue of regional supply chains: they manufacture components, distribute goods, provide last-mile delivery, and fulfill contracted services on which larger companies depend. Yet despite their critical role, the vast majority of small businesses operate without formalized logistics management systems, standardized distribution procedures, or professional-quality inventory controls.

This operational gap creates a cascading vulnerability: when small businesses cannot reliably coordinate deliveries, manage inventory turnover, or optimize contract fulfillment, the broader supply networks they serve experience delays, cost overruns, and service failures that propagate through entire regional economies.

The magnitude of this problem is not theoretical. The Federal Reserve's 2023 Small Business Credit Survey found that 43% of employer small businesses reported financial challenges in 2022, and 26% had difficulty accessing credit (Federal Reserve Banks, "2023 Small Business Credit Survey," https://www.fedsmallbusiness.org). These financial constraints directly limit small businesses' ability to invest in logistics infrastructure—route optimization software, inventory management systems, warehouse coordination tools, and trained logistics personnel—that larger competitors deploy as standard practice.

The result is a two-tier supply chain ecosystem in which large companies operate with sophisticated logistics capabilities, while small businesses—which collectively employ nearly half of the private workforce—rely on ad hoc, informal, and often inefficient distribution methods that increase per-unit costs, elevate delivery failure rates, and reduce the overall resilience of the supply networks in which they participate.

Current macroeconomic conditions are intensifying these pressures. According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, the Producer Price Index (PPI) for final demand advanced 0.5% in March 2026 alone, with goods prices increasing 1.6% while services remained unchanged (BLS, Homepage Announcements, 2026, https://www.bls.gov/).

During the preceding twelve-month period, prices for final demand increased 4.0% for the twelve months ended March 2026 (BLS, Homepage Announcements, 2026, https://www.bls.gov/). For small businesses operating on thin margins without professional logistics systems to control costs, these sustained input price increases translate directly into compressed profitability, reduced capacity for investment, and greater vulnerability to supply disruptions.

Unlike large companies that can absorb cost increases through volume purchasing, hedging strategies, and centralized logistics optimization, small businesses without standardized logistics procedures face these price pressures without a systematic mechanism for cost containment.

The labor market context compounds the challenge. The Bureau of Labor Statistics reported that payroll employment increased by 178,000 in March 2026, with the unemployment rate changing little at 4.3% (BLS, Homepage Announcements, 2026, https://www.bls.gov/). A tight labor market means that small businesses face elevated competition for workers—including logistics coordinators, warehouse personnel, and delivery staff—increasing labor costs precisely when input prices are already rising.

Simultaneously, average real hourly earnings for all employees decreased by 0.6% in March 2026 (BLS, Homepage Announcements, 2026, https://www.bls.gov/), indicating that inflation is outpacing wage growth and further constraining the purchasing power of the workforce on which small businesses depend. This combination of rising input costs, tight labor conditions, and declining real wages creates an environment in which small businesses without efficient and standardized logistics systems are increasingly unable to maintain delivery reliability, control operating expenses, or retain the workforce necessary for consistent supply chain participation.

The Consumer Price Index for all items increased 0.9% in March 2026, with gasoline prices rising notably (BLS, Homepage Announcements, 2026, https://www.bls.gov/). For small businesses involved in distribution, delivery, and contracted services—the precise segment the proposed project targets—fuel cost volatility represents a direct operational threat.

Without standardized route optimization procedures, consolidated delivery scheduling, or systematic fuel cost management protocols, logistics-dependent small businesses absorb these price shocks inefficiently, often passing costs to customers in ways that reduce competitiveness or absorbing them internally in ways that erode financial viability.

2.2 Why Existing Solutions Are Insufficient

The logistics consulting industry in the United States is overwhelmingly oriented toward serving mid-sized and large companies. Major consulting firms—including supply chain practices at firms like McKinsey, Deloitte, and Accenture—design their service models around engagements valued in the hundreds of thousands to millions of dollars, with implementation timelines measured in quarters or years and staffing models that assume dedicated project management teams on the client side.

These service models are structurally inaccessible to small businesses with fewer than 100 employees, annual revenues below $5 million, and no internal logistics department. The result is a persistent service gap: the companies that most need logistics standardization are precisely those least able to access professional logistics consulting.

Software-based solutions, while increasingly available, do not address the core problem. Enterprise resource planning (ERP) platforms, transportation management systems (TMS), and warehouse management systems (WMS) have proliferated in recent years, but these tools require significant capital investment, technical expertise for implementation, and ongoing IT support for maintenance—resources that small businesses typically lack.

According to the SBA Office of Advocacy, small businesses created 12.9 million net new jobs between 1995 and 2020, representing 63% of new job creation (SBA Office of Advocacy, Frequently Asked Questions 2023, https://advocacy.sba.gov). These job-creating businesses need operating systems that fit their scale, budget, and staffing realities—not scaled-down versions of enterprise platforms designed for organizations with dedicated IT departments and six-figure technology budgets.

Government programs aimed at small business development—including those administered through the SBA's Small Business Development Centers (SBDCs), SCORE mentoring networks, and Minority Business Development Agency (MBDA) centers—provide valuable general business guidance but do not offer the specialized, hands-on logistics implementation services that small businesses require.

These programs typically provide advisory consultations, business plan reviews, and financial guidance, but do not deploy consultants to design standardized operating procedures for distribution coordination, create inventory management protocols tailored to specific business operations, or implement contract services optimization frameworks on-site.

The gap between general business advice and logistics operational implementation remains unaddressed by existing federal and state programs.

These inefficiencies do not remain contained within individual businesses—they propagate through supply networks, causing delays for downstream customers, increasing costs for upstream suppliers, and reducing the overall reliability of regional supply chains.

2.2 Technical Description of the Solution

The consulting methodology that Mr. Contreras will deploy through Contreras Supply Logistics LLC follows a structured seven-step technical workflow developed from his more than 15 years of experience managing logistics operations, coordinating distribution for more than 50 clients, and implementing systems that produced measurable efficiency gains in service-oriented businesses. Each step is designed to produce a specific and documented deliverable that the client retains and operates independently after the engagement concludes—ensuring that the logistics infrastructure persists beyond the consulting relationship and becomes an integrated operational asset for the business.

Step 1: Operational Logistics Diagnostic Assessment

Each engagement begins with a comprehensive diagnostic assessment of the client's existing logistics operations. Mr. Contreras conducts on-site and remote evaluations of the client's current distribution workflows, inventory management practices, supplier relationships and contracts, order fulfillment processes, and delivery performance metrics. This assessment uses a standardized diagnostic framework that Mr. Contreras developed during his tenure as Logistics Coordinator at Cooperativa Servicios y Suministros Zulia, where he managed distribution logistics for more than 50 clients and identified recurring patterns of operational failures across diverse small business environments. The diagnostic produces a quantified baseline report that documents current on-time delivery rates, distribution costs per unit, inventory turnover ratios, order error rates, and contract utilization efficiency—establishing the measurable starting point against which all subsequent improvements are tracked.

Step 2: Supply Chain Vulnerability Mapping

Building on the diagnostic baseline, Mr. Contreras maps the client's supply chain vulnerabilities—identifying single points of failure in supplier relationships, geographic concentration risks in distribution routes, inventory buffer insufficiencies, and contractual gaps that expose the business to service disruptions. This step is based on the petitioner's experience coordinating procurement and inventory management at Cooperativa Servicios y Suministros Zulia, where he reduced operational costs by 15% by identifying and eliminating specific supply chain inefficiencies.

The vulnerability map categorizes each identified risk by probability, potential financial impact, and remediation priority, producing a ranked action plan that directs subsequent system design toward the highest-impact improvements first.

Step 3: Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) Development

Mr. Contreras designs a comprehensive set of standardized operating procedures tailored to the client's specific product mix, order volume, workforce size, and distribution geography. These SOPs cover five operational domains: (a) inbound logistics and receiving protocols, (b) inventory storage, tracking, and replenishment procedures, (c) order processing and fulfillment workflows, (d) outbound distribution and delivery coordination, and (e) supplier communication and contract management processes.

Each SOP document specifies responsible personnel, required inputs, step-by-step execution instructions, quality control checkpoints, exception handling procedures, and performance metrics. This approach directly replicates the methodology Mr. Contreras employed at Cooperativa Servicios y Suministros Zulia, where his development of standardized operating procedures improved team productivity by 25%—a result achieved by eliminating process ambiguity and establishing clear accountability at each operational node.

Step 4: Inventory Management Protocol Design

For each client, Mr. Contreras designs an inventory management protocol calibrated to the business's specific demand patterns, supplier lead times, storage capacity, and cash flow constraints. The protocol establishes reorder points, safety stock levels, inventory classification systems (using ABC analysis adapted for small business scale), and cycle counting schedules that replace the intuition-based informal inventory practices that characterize most small business operations.

The protocol is designed to be executable using tools already available to small businesses—including spreadsheet-based tracking systems, low-cost inventory management software, and manual counting procedures—rather than requiring enterprise-level technology investments that small businesses cannot afford. This practical constraint is central to the project's design philosophy: each system must be implementable within the client's existing resource environment.

Step 5: Distribution Coordination Framework Implementation

Mr. Contreras implements a distribution coordination framework that optimizes the client's delivery routes, carrier relationships, shipment consolidation practices, and delivery scheduling. This framework addresses the specific challenge small businesses face when managing distribution without dedicated logistics personnel: inconsistent carrier selection, suboptimal route planning, missed consolidation opportunities, and reactive rather than proactive delivery scheduling.

The framework produces documented carrier evaluation criteria, route optimization guides, shipment batching protocols, and delivery performance tracking dashboards that enable existing client personnel to manage distribution operations to a professional standard. During his leadership of logistics operations for more than 50 clients at Cooperativa Servicios y Suministros Zulia, Mr. Contreras developed and refined these distribution coordination techniques, ensuring timely and efficient deliveries across a diverse client base with variable geographic and volume requirements.

Step 6: Contract Services Optimization

Mr. Contreras reviews and restructures the client's existing supplier and service contracts to eliminate redundancies, renegotiate unfavorable terms, consolidate fragmented agreements, and establish performance-based accountability provisions. Many small businesses operate under legacy contracts that were negotiated without logistics expertise, resulting in above-market rates, inadequate service level agreements, and misaligned payment terms that strain cash flow.

The contract optimization process produces a consolidated supplier management plan with standardized evaluation criteria, renewal calendars, performance benchmarks, and escalation procedures—transforming contract management from an administrative burden into a strategic logistics function.

Step 7: Personnel Training, Knowledge Transfer, and Sustainability Audit

Each engagement concludes with a structured knowledge transfer program that trains existing client personnel to operate, maintain, and continuously improve the logistics systems installed during the engagement. Mr. Contreras conducts hands-on training sessions covering each SOP, inventory protocol, distribution framework, and contract management procedure, supplemented by written training manuals and quick-reference guides.

A sustainability audit conducted at 60 and 120 days post-implementation verifies that systems are functioning as designed, identifies any operational drift, and provides corrective guidance. This step ensures that the logistics infrastructure becomes a permanent operational capability of the client's business rather than a temporary improvement that degrades after the consultant departs.

Mr. Contreras's experience supervising teams of 10 or more professionals at Kafanda Business and Service LLC, where he fostered collaborative and results-oriented work environments, directly informs his approach to training non-specialist personnel in systematic logistics procedures.

Federal policy has recognized supply chain resilience as a national priority. The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (Pub. Mr. Contreras's project addresses this operational layer: by equipping 250-350 small businesses over five years with professional-level logistics systems, the project strengthens the distributed network of firms that collectively constitute the nation's regional supply chains. Each client that achieves improved on-time delivery performance, reduced distribution costs, and more resilient supplier relationships becomes a more reliable node in its regional supply network.

Pathway 3: Reducing Small Business Failure Through Operational Improvement

According to the SBA Office of Advocacy's Frequently Asked Questions 2023 (https://advocacy.sba.gov), small businesses created 12.9 million net new jobs between 1995 and 2020, representing 63% of new job creation. The survival and growth of these businesses is therefore a direct determinant of U.S. employment levels. Logistics inefficiency—manifested as excessive distribution costs, inventory waste, delivery failures, and unfavorable contract terms—directly erodes the financial margins on which small businesses depend to survive.

By reducing operational costs by up to 15% and improving operational efficiency by up to 30% for each client, Mr. Contreras's project strengthens the financial viability of small businesses that collectively employ millions of U.S. workers. The projected aggregate client economic benefit of $8-12 million according to independent analysis over five years represents capital that remains within the small business sector rather than being lost to logistics inefficiency—capital that supports job retention, business expansion, and continued economic contribution.

Unlike consulting models that create ongoing dependency, Mr. Contreras's methodology is explicitly designed to produce client self-sufficiency. 6.1 Impracticability of Labor Certification for the Proposed Effort

The Program Electronic Review Management (PERM) labor certification process, codified at 20 C.F.R. § 656, is designed to test the U.S. labor market for a specific position offered by a specific employer at a specific work location. The process requires the sponsoring employer to demonstrate, through a prescribed recruitment campaign, that no qualified, willing, and able U.S. worker is available for the position as described.

This framework presupposes a traditional employer-employee relationship in which the beneficiary will occupy a single defined role within an established organizational hierarchy. The proposed effort—Small Business Logistics Management for U.S. Supply Resilience, operated through Contreras Supply Logistics LLC—is fundamentally incompatible with this framework for several structural reasons.

First, Mr. Contreras is the founder and principal of the entity, not an employee of a sponsoring employer. Contreras Supply Logistics LLC is a Florida-based consulting firm that Mr. Contreras will establish and operate as its chief executive. There is no separate employer to sponsor a PERM application on his behalf. The Department of Labor's labor certification regulations do not contemplate a scenario in which the beneficiary and the employer are the same person, since the self-petitioning entrepreneur cannot simultaneously serve as the sponsoring employer conducting a good-faith recruitment test for his or her own position.

This structural impossibility is precisely the type of circumstance the AAO contemplated when it noted in Dhanasar that "labor certification is designed for a specific type of employment arrangement" and that the waiver may be appropriate when the petitioner's proposed endeavor does not fit within that arrangement.

Second, the effort requires simultaneous service to multiple independent clients, not a single employer. As detailed in Section V, Contreras Supply Logistics LLC projects serving 12-15 active clients in Year 1, scaling to 100-120 active clients by Year 5, with a cumulative client base of 250-350 small businesses during the five-year projection period. Each client engagement involves a distinct scope of work—logistics assessment, standard operating procedure development, distribution coordination optimization, and inventory management protocol implementation—adapted to each small business's specific operational realities.

No single PERM application could capture the multi-client, multi-engagement nature of this work, since each client relationship constitutes a separate service delivery context with distinct requirements, timelines, and deliverables.

Third, the petitioner's role spans multiple Standard Occupational Classification (SOC) codes, challenging PERM's single-position framework. The work Mr. Contreras will perform through Contreras Supply Logistics LLC encompasses functions classified under at least three distinct SOC codes: SOC 13-1081 (Logisticians), which covers supply chain operations analysis and coordination; SOC 11-1021 (General and Operations Managers), which covers organizational operations supervision and resource allocation; and SOC 13-1111 (Management Analysts), which covers the consulting function of advising organizations on operational efficiency.

The PERM process requires the employer to select a single SOC code and recruit for a single job description. Mr. Contreras's effort, by its nature, requires the integrated application of competencies spanning all three classifications, a reality that cannot be reduced to a single job description without materially misrepresenting the scope and nature of the work.

Fourth, the effort's geographic scope and client acquisition model are incompatible with PERM's worksite requirements. The labor certification process requires specification of a primary worksite. Contreras Supply Logistics LLC will serve small businesses across major Florida metropolitan corridors—including Miami-Dade, Broward, Palm Beach, Hillsborough, Orange, and Duval counties—and will expand regionally as documented in Phase 3 and Phase 4 implementation milestones (Section III).

Client engagements will be conducted both on-site at client facilities and remotely, with no fixed secondary worksite. The fluid, multi-location nature of consulting engagements makes PERM's worksite designation requirement impractical and potentially misleading.

For these reasons—the absence of a separate sponsoring employer, the multi-client service model, the cross-classification nature of the work, and the multi-location delivery framework—the labor certification process is structurally impracticable for the proposed effort. Requiring Mr. Contreras to obtain labor certification would effectively prevent the establishment of Contreras Supply Logistics LLC, eliminating the national benefits documented throughout this plan.

This tension is inherent to any logistics consulting practice serving the small business sector, where the U.S. Small Business Administration's 2023 Small Business Profile documents that small businesses (fewer than 500 employees) represent 99.9% of all U.S. firms (SBA Office of Advocacy, 2023 Small Business Profile, https://advocacy.sba.gov/2023/03/07/2023-small-business-profile/)—a population characterized by extraordinary operational diversity.

Probability: High. Probability is assessed as High because client heterogeneity is not a contingent risk but a structural feature of the small business logistics consulting market. Each client engagement will require some degree of customization, and the firm will inevitably encounter legacy systems and practices that resist standardization. Mr. Contreras's experience managing logistics for more than 50 clients at Cooperativa Servicios y Suministros Zulia—where he developed standardized operating procedures that improved team productivity by 25% despite serving clients with varied operational profiles—provides direct evidence of his ability to navigate this challenge, but the risk remains inherent to the venture's operating model.

Potential Impact: If standardization proves more time-intensive than projected, average engagement duration could increase from the planned 8-12 weeks to 14-18 weeks, reducing the firm's annual client throughput by 20-30%. Under this scenario, Year 1 active clients could fall from the projected 12-15 to 8-10, with proportional reductions in revenue. Additionally, excessive customization could erode the scalability of the firm's SOP templates, requiring Mr. Contreras to invest additional hours in custom framework development rather than deploying replicable modules—a dynamic that would increase delivery costs per client and compress margins.

Mitigation Strategy: Mr. Contreras will invest $8,000 from Phase 1 and Phase 3 capital allocations ($4,000 from each phase) in the development of a tiered standardization architecture comprising three layers. Layer 1 - Universal Core: A set of 12-15 logistics management modules applicable to any small business regardless of sector, covering fundamental processes such as order entry documentation, delivery scheduling protocols, supplier payment tracking, and inventory cycle counting procedures.

These modules will constitute approximately 60% of any client engagement and will be deployed without modification. Layer 2 - Sector-Specific Modules: Pre-built customization packages for the firm's four primary target sectors (food distribution, construction supply, retail inventory, light manufacturing), each containing 4-6 additional modules addressing sector-specific regulatory requirements, perishability constraints, or supplier qualification standards.

These modules will constitute approximately 25% of any engagement. Layer 3 - Client-Specific Customization: A reserved allocation of 15% of each engagement's scope for bespoke adjustments that accommodate unique legacy systems or operational practices that cannot be addressed by Layers 1 or 2. By pre-building Layers 1 and 2 during the Phase 1 development period (Months 1-6), Mr. Contreras will reduce the per-client customization burden and maintain engagement durations within the projected 8-12 week range for 80% or more of client engagements.

Timeline: Layer 1 (Universal Core) module development will be completed during Months 1-4 as part of Phase 1 capital deployment. Layer 2 (Sector-Specific) modules for the first two target sectors (food distribution and construction supply) will be completed by Month 6; modules for retail inventory and light manufacturing will be developed during Months 7-12 as the firm gains pilot client data from these sectors.

Layer 3 customization protocols will be documented by Month 5 and refined continuously as the firm accumulates client engagement experience. The tiered architecture will be fully operational by Month 12, enabling the firm to maintain projected client throughput rates starting in Year 2.

Risk 5: Economic Recession Reducing Consumer Demand and Freight Volumes, Thus Reducing the Potential Client Base for Small Business Logistics Services

Nature and Description: A macroeconomic recession—characterized by declining GDP, reduced consumer spending, and contracting freight volumes—would directly reduce the demand for logistics consulting services among small businesses. During economic downturns, small businesses prioritize survival over operational optimization, often cutting discretionary spending on external consulting services even when those services could improve efficiency and reduce costs.

The Federal Reserve's monetary policy stance and current macroeconomic indicators suggest elevated recession risk during the 2025-2027 period. According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, the Producer Price Index for final demand increased 4.0% for the twelve months ended March 2026 (BLS, Homepage Announcements, 2026, https://www.bls.gov/), reflecting sustained inflationary pressure that historically precedes Federal Reserve interest rate increases. Higher interest rates increase borrowing costs for small businesses, compress margins, and reduce the capital available for investments in operational improvements—including logistics consulting.

Probability: Medium. Probability is assessed as Medium rather than High or Low because while macroeconomic cycles are inevitable, the timing and severity of any recession during the 2025-2030 period remains uncertain. Economic forecasting models show divergent predictions, and the Federal Reserve's policy responses to inflation may successfully engineer a "soft landing" that avoids recession. However, the sustained elevation in input costs documented in Section II creates recessionary pressure that cannot be dismissed.

Potential Impact: A moderate recession occurring during Years 2-3 of the implementation timeline could reduce client acquisition rates by 30-40%, lowering Year 2 revenues from the projected $145,000 to approximately $87,000-$101,000 and delaying break-even to Year 3. More severely, a deep recession during Years 1-2 could reduce Year 1 client acquisition to 6-8 clients (rather than the projected 12-15), generating approximately $32,000-$42,000 in Year 1 revenue—insufficient to sustain operations beyond Month 18 without additional capital injection.

The indirect effect would extend to the effort's projected national benefit: fewer clients served means proportionally lower cumulative client economic benefit, delayed job creation, and reduced contribution to small business supply chain resilience.

Mitigation Strategy: Mr. Contreras will implement a three-component recession-resilience strategy budgeted at $3,800 in Year 1 and scaling to $6,500 annually in Years 2-5. First, the firm will develop a recession-oriented value proposition emphasizing that logistics optimization produces immediate cost reductions that are most valuable precisely when businesses face revenue pressure—repositioning consulting services from discretionary improvement to essential cost containment. Marketing materials, case studies, and client presentations will be adapted to emphasize documented cost reduction outcomes (15% operational cost reduction based on petitioner's prior experience) rather than growth-oriented benefits.

Second, the firm will establish a flexible engagement model that offers reduced-scope "essential logistics audit" services at $2,500-$3,500 for clients unable to commit to full engagement fees during economic downturns. These reduced-scope engagements will focus exclusively on the highest-impact cost reduction opportunities identified in diagnostic assessment, providing immediate client value while maintaining the firm's revenue pipeline at reduced margins.

Third, Mr. Contreras will proactively build relationships with economic development agencies and workforce development boards that administer small business support programs funded by state and federal sources—including potential SBA disaster relief programs, state-level economic resilience grants, and workforce training subsidies. During recessions, these programs often expand funding to support small business operational improvements, creating opportunities for Contreras Supply Logistics LLC to serve as a preferred logistics consulting provider for subsidized programs.

Timeline: Recession-oriented messaging will be integrated into all marketing materials developed during Phase 1 (Months 1-6). The reduced-scope engagement model will be documented and priced by Month 4, ready for immediate activation if macroeconomic indicators signal recession onset. Economic development agency outreach will begin in Month 6 and continue throughout the five-year period, with formal partnership or preferred provider applications submitted during Months 9-12 to position the firm for potential subsidized program work starting in Year 2.

The endeavor has substantial merit and national importance because it addresses a documented vulnerability in U.S. small business supply chain capacity that affects 99.9% of U.S. firms employing 45.9% of the private workforce, a vulnerability that federal policy has recognized through $1.2 trillion in infrastructure investment and explicit executive orders on supply chain resilience.

The petitioner is well positioned to advance this endeavor through more than 15 years of documented professional experience managing logistics operations for more than 50 clients, developing procedures that produced measurable 15-30% operational improvements, operating a U.S. business since 2021, and committing $110,000 in phased capital deployment with detailed implementation milestones.

On balance, it benefits the United States to waive labor certification requirements because the entrepreneurial nature of the endeavor is structurally incompatible with the PERM process, the petitioner's distinctive methodology provides national benefits that would be lost absent the waiver, and the urgency of strengthening small business supply chain resilience—combined with the creation of 5-6 direct U.S. jobs and $8-12 million in projected client economic benefit—outweighs the value of requiring labor certification for a position that cannot exist within the traditional employer-employee framework.

The United States small business sector requires immediate access to professional logistics systems to participate effectively in national supply chains and capitalize on federal infrastructure investments. Mr. Contreras's proposal delivers these systems through a financially viable, operationally grounded, and nationally beneficial business model. Approval of this petition will enable the timely creation of U.S. jobs, the strengthening of U.S. supply chains, and the advancement of documented federal policy priorities.
